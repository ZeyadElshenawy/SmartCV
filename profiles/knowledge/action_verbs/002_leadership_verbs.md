# Leadership and Management Action Verbs

Leadership bullets describe managing people, owning outcomes, and influencing decisions. The MIT, Indeed, and Muse compilations agree on the core set; the differences are mostly in granularity.

**People management:**
Coached, Cultivated, Developed (people), Directed, Hired, Mentored, Motivated, Onboarded, Promoted, Recruited, Reviewed, Supervised, Trained.

**Project / initiative ownership:**
Chaired, Coordinated, Delegated, Drove, Executed, Headed, Led, Managed, Orchestrated, Organized, Oversaw, Owned, Planned, Prioritized, Scheduled.

**Decision / direction:**
Aligned, Authorized, Decided, Endorsed, Established, Formalized, Recommended, Resolved, Steered.

**Cross-functional influence:**
Advocated, Aligned, Championed, Collaborated, Convened, Influenced, Negotiated, Partnered, Persuaded, Unified.

**Organizational change:**
Centralized, Consolidated, Implemented, Integrated, Merged, Reorganized, Restructured, Standardized, Transformed.

**Verbs to avoid in leadership bullets:**
- "Spearheaded" — overused buzzword (banned in `prompt_guards.py`).
- "Empowered" — vague; describe the actual mechanism (mentored, delegated, removed a blocker).
- "Drove transformation" — empty corporate-speak unless followed by the specific change.
- "Took ownership of" — say what you did with the ownership.
- "Liaised with" — say "worked with", "negotiated with", or "represented X to Y".

**Pairing leadership verbs with scale metrics.**
A leadership bullet without a scale number reads weak. Always include team size, budget, audience, or scope.

- "Led a team of 6 engineers across 2 timezones to ship the billing rewrite, hitting the Q3 deadline 2 weeks early."
- "Mentored 4 junior engineers; 3 were promoted to mid-level within 18 months."
- "Coordinated cross-functional launch with PM, design, and 3 vendor teams; cut launch coordination meetings from 5/week to 1."

**Common scale signals to attach:**
- Team size: "team of N", "N direct reports", "across N teams".
- Budget: "$N annual budget", "owned $N quarterly spend".
- Audience: "presented to N executives", "trained N hires".
- Scope: "across N business units", "spanning N countries".

**Seniority calibration.** "Led" should be reserved for situations where you actually had decision-making authority. A junior engineer who participated in a project should write "Contributed to" or "Helped ship", not "Led". Inflated leadership claims at the junior level are a major red flag for senior recruiters and easy to verify in references.

For senior roles, the leadership verbs should appear at least 2–3 times across the most recent role's bullets. For mid-level, 1–2. For junior or intern, 0–1 (unless genuinely leading a small project).

## Concrete rule for SmartCV

Use leadership verbs (Led, Managed, Coordinated, Mentored, etc.) only when the source CV explicitly indicates the candidate had ownership or people-management responsibility. Always pair leadership verbs with a scale number (team size, budget, audience, or scope). For junior and intern resumes, prefer "Contributed to", "Helped ship", or "Collaborated on" over "Led" unless the source CV documents actual leadership of a project.
