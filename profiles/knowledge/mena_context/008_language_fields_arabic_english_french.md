# Language Fields — Arabic, English, French Conventions

The Languages section is more load-bearing on a MENA CV than on a US one, because the regional labour market is genuinely multilingual and the candidate's specific language mix changes which roles they qualify for.

**Mashreq (Egypt, Saudi Arabia, UAE, Kuwait, Qatar, Bahrain, Oman, Jordan, Lebanon, Syria, Iraq, Palestine, Yemen):**
- Arabic is the official language. Egypt's official language is Modern Standard Arabic ("Arabic" with "Modern Standard Arabic" as the official register and "Egyptian Arabic" as the national variety).
- English is the de facto language of business in the Gulf — Wikipedia notes for the UAE: "Arabic is the official language while English remains the most spoken language and the language of business."
- The candidate is expected to declare proficiency in **Arabic AND English** at minimum.

**Maghreb (Morocco, Algeria, Tunisia):**
- Arabic and Berber are the official languages. In Morocco "the official languages of Morocco are Modern Standard Arabic and Standard Moroccan Amazigh." In Algeria "the official languages of Algeria are Arabic and Standard Algerian Amazigh."
- French is the practical language of business and higher education. Wikipedia notes for Morocco: "French... is often used for business, diplomacy, and government; and serves as a lingua franca" and "serves as Morocco's primary language of commerce and economics, culture, sciences and medicine." For Algeria: "French continues to be the dominant language in business and professional circles" and "All scientific and business university courses are still taught in French."
- The candidate is expected to declare **Arabic, French, and English** — usually in that order of regional weight, though English is rising fast for tech roles.

Proficiency scales:

- The European-origin **CEFR scale** (A1–C2 — Wikipedia: "C2 (Proficient User): Can 'understand with ease virtually everything heard or read'") is used by some MENA candidates with European-style education (GUC, AUC, French-system schools). It carries weight at multinationals.
- The **descriptive scale** (Native / Fluent / Advanced / Intermediate / Basic) is far more common on local-employer CVs and on Wuzzuf / Bayt structured profiles.

## Concrete rule for SmartCV

Always include a Languages section on MENA CVs (it is rarely omitted). Default to the descriptive scale (Native / Fluent / Advanced / Intermediate / Basic) for local-employer targets and the CEFR scale (A1–C2) for multinational and EU-headquartered targets. Order languages by regional weight: for Mashreq targets, Arabic → English → others; for Maghreb targets, Arabic → French → English → others. Always include the candidate's native language explicitly with the label "Native" — do not assume Arabic is implied. If the candidate speaks Berber/Tamazight, include it (it is constitutionally official in Morocco and Algeria).

## What public sources document

**Language directly affects pay in Egypt** — confirmed by Wuzzuf's own salary analytics (`wuzzuf.net/careers/how-language-skills-can-increase-your-salary/` and `how-language-decides-customer-service-salary/`, accessed 2026-05-12 via search excerpts): Arabic-only entry-level pay averages ~EGP 5,500/month, English-fluent pay averages ~EGP 13,000/month (a ~2.4× multiplier on identical work), and French-fluent roles in the same industries can reach EGP 27,000/month. The Languages section is therefore not cosmetic on an Egyptian CV — it is a salary-bracket determinant.

**Maghreb language expectations** are confirmed across multiple sources (accessed 2026-05-12 via search excerpts):
- Morocco: French "dominates corporate roles such as mining and banking"; "in most government offices, banks, and many other contexts, you are expected to know either French or Arabic" (Crossroads Cultural Exchange, accessed 2026-05-12). CV-advice sources (resumeflex.com, resume-example.com, accessed 2026-05-12) recommend the CV itself be in French or Arabic, with English acceptable only at multinationals and in IT/tourism.
- Formal certifications "**DELF (French), IELTS (English), or DELE (Spanish) add credibility**" on Maghreb CVs (resume-example.com, accessed 2026-05-12).

The CEFR scale (A1–C2) is the standard European framework used by multinationals and EU-headquartered firms (Wikipedia, *Common European Framework of Reference for Languages*, accessed 2026-05-12 — already cited in this file's headline section).

I could not directly verify Wuzzuf's structured language-proficiency dropdown values (Native / Fluent / Good / Fair / Basic) because the platform's profile-builder UI is gated behind a login and the help-center articles redirect to blocked URLs. The Bayt blog excerpt mentions language fields as part of profile completion but does not specify the dropdown values.
