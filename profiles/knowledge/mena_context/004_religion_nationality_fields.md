# Religion and Nationality Fields on MENA CVs

Two fields that would trigger anti-discrimination review in a US or UK CV — **religion** and **nationality** — are both expected and structurally required in many MENA hiring contexts.

**Nationality** is effectively mandatory in the Gulf because of nationalisation quotas. Saudi Arabia's Saudization (Nitaqat) policy "is implemented in the Kingdom of Saudi Arabia" and requires "companies and enterprises to fill their workforce with Saudi nationals up to certain levels." It was formally announced through "Ministerial Resolution no. (4040)" in June 2011 and classifies firms into "Platinum, High Green, Mid Green, Low Green, Yellow, and Red," with sanctions: "Companies that failed to comply with Saudization regulations will not be awarded government contracts." Similar nationalisation programmes (Emiratisation, Qatarisation, Omanisation, Bahrainisation, Kuwaitisation) operate in the rest of the Gulf, all of which require employers to track headcount by nationality. Demographic context: Wikipedia notes UAE is roughly 89% expatriate ("Only 11% of the population are native Emiratis"), and Saudi Arabia has "the world's third-largest immigrant population, with foreign-born residents comprising roughly 40% of the population." Nationality on the CV is what lets a Gulf recruiter immediately route the application.

**Religion** is more variable. It appears on some Gulf and Levant applications (especially government-linked or family-owned firms) but is largely absent from multinational subsidiaries and from Egyptian private-sector roles posted by international companies. There is no public source mandating religion on a CV; its presence is cultural rather than legal.

## Concrete rule for SmartCV

Always include a **Nationality** field on MENA-targeted CVs (Egypt, Saudi Arabia, UAE, Kuwait, Qatar, Bahrain, Oman, Jordan, Lebanon, Morocco, Tunisia, Algeria). Use the candidate's passport nationality verbatim (e.g., "Egyptian", "Saudi Arabian", "Lebanese-Canadian"). For Gulf-targeted CVs, also surface "Visa Status" if known (e.g., "Iqama transferable", "Family-sponsored", "GCC national"). Do NOT auto-add a Religion field. Only include religion if the candidate has explicitly entered it in their profile AND the target job is in a Gulf or Levant market. Never infer religion from name, nationality, or photo.

## What public sources document

**Nationality** is confirmed as both a CV field and an active recruiter search-filter on Bayt (Bayt employer help center, accessed 2026-05-12). Bayt's CV-search tool lets recruiters include or exclude specific nationalities.

**Visa Status** is treated as a distinct field separate from Nationality on Gulf CVs. Cross-source guidance (art2write.com, gulfcareerlink.com, tailormycv.app, visualcv.com Gulf-CV guide, accessed 2026-05-12 via search excerpts) converges on a small set of canonical values:

- "Employment Visa – Transferable"
- "Spouse Visa – NOC Available"
- "Iqama: Transferable" / "Transferable Iqama – No NOC required"
- "Visit Visa"
- "Resident Visa"

The phrase "**Transferable Iqama – No NOC required**" is repeatedly highlighted across these sources as the one that signals fastest time-to-hire to Gulf recruiters. NOC = No Objection Certificate from the current sponsor.

**Religion** as a CV field appears across MENA-CV advice content with the same split the file describes: external guidance (resumewritinglab.com, careerhigher.co, visualcv.com guides, accessed 2026-05-12 via search excerpts) and academic research on hiring discrimination (Di Stasio et al., *Journal of Ethnic and Migration Studies*, 2019) consistently advise candidates targeting multinationals or international ATS pipelines to **omit religion** on the CV, while noting that **Saudi-Arabia visa and employment application forms** themselves ask about religion. The distinction the file makes — religion is cultural/form-driven, not legally mandated on CVs — is supported by public sources.

I could not verify whether Wuzzuf or Bayt collect religion as a structured profile field; direct fetches to wuzzuf.net and bayt.com profile-builder pages were blocked (HTTP 403).
